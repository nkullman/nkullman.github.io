About me

I grew up in St. Louis and attended the University of Missouri where I earned a B.S. in physics. During my time at Mizzou, I got my hands on as many different types of projects as I could, including alternative fuels, geospatial analysis, optics, and exoplanets. I also co-founded the university's math club with the girl who would later become my wife.

After graduation, we moved to Kansas City where I worked with Sprint as a telecom design engineer. I was fortunate to be with Sprint during a big network overhaul, which afforded me opportunities to learn about the latest in wireless communications and to test and deploy new equipment. I also amassed almost 20 patents during my time with Sprint.

I left to pursue graduate education in 2013, aiming to further my quantitative training and analytics abilities. The University of Washington's Quantitative Ecology and Resource Management (QERM) program was a perfect fit. I now work under the supervision of Sándor Tóth, developing optimization models and visualization tools. Our models are tailored to address questions in climate change and ecosystem services. You can read more about them on my research page.

In 2016, I also had the opportunity to spend a few months developing optimization models for electric vehicle routing with a research group based in Tours, France. More on my eVRO work here.

Outside work, my wife and I like to spend time outside and around food.

About the QERM graduate program

Quantitative Ecology & Resource Management (QERM) is a unique interdisciplinary graduate program designed for students interested in applying quantitative tools to ecological and resource management issues. Students receive intensive training in the application of statistical, mathematical, and decision sciences to terrestrial and aquatic ecology, natural resource management, biometrics, and mathematical biology.

Check out the QERM network viz, or see what other QERMies and QERM faculty are up to on their student and faculty webpages.
